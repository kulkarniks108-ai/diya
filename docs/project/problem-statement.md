# Problem Statement and Urgency

## Core Problem

Visually impaired individuals need continuous, real-time awareness of their surroundings and reliable safety support to navigate daily life independently. Most existing assistive solutions force an unfair trade-off: systems with strong capability are often too expensive, while affordable options are too limited to provide meaningful independence.

This leaves many people without practical tools to understand their environment, move confidently, and stay safe.

## Who Experiences This Problem

Primary affected users:
- Blind and visually impaired individuals

Secondary affected users:
- Family members and caregivers who carry ongoing safety anxiety

## Where and When the Problem Is Most Severe

The challenge is most visible in situations that require fast contextual awareness and confidence:
- Roads, intersections, and crowded outdoor movement
- Public spaces and unfamiliar environments
- Campuses and workplaces where navigation speed matters
- Social contexts where identifying people or understanding context is difficult
- Low-connectivity areas where internet-dependent assistive systems fail

## Why This Must Be Addressed Now

Millions of visually impaired people are still blocked by a cost-capability divide that directly affects mobility, education, employment, and social participation.

This is especially urgent now because:
- Smartphones are already widely available and can act as assistive compute platforms
- AI capability has advanced enough to deliver practical real-time assistance
- Modular hardware can be added incrementally rather than requiring a large upfront investment

A smartphone-centered, modular assistive model can remove financial barriers while preserving long-term functionality and user dignity.

## Structural Gaps in Existing Solutions

1. Cost barrier: advanced systems remain inaccessible for many users.
2. Capability barrier: low-cost options often provide narrow functionality.
3. Connectivity assumptions: many AI tools degrade sharply without stable internet.
4. Device assumptions: many solutions require specific premium hardware.
5. Reliability gap: failure and reconnection behavior is often not robust enough for daily dependence.

## What Success Looks Like at the Problem Level

A successful platform should make assistive capability:
- Practical in daily life
- Affordable at entry level
- Expandable over time
- Reliable in non-ideal conditions
- Usable with minimal ongoing user effort

The objective is not just feature delivery, but real independence, confidence, and safety continuity.
